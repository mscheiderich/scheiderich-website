Georgia's State Minimum Limits Are Not Enough — And the Consequences Are Personal

Georgia requires drivers to carry a minimum of $25,000 in bodily injury liability per person. That sounds like a meaningful number until you consider what a serious accident actually costs. A single emergency room visit, surgery, and follow-up care can exceed that limit before a person even leaves the hospital.

Here is what most drivers do not fully understand: liability insurance covers the other party's losses when you are at fault — up to your policy limit. Everything beyond that limit becomes your personal responsibility. The injured party's attorney does not stop at your insurance limit. They can pursue a lawsuit against you directly. They can garnish your wages. They can place a lien on your home. They can go after your savings.

This is not a rare edge case. It is a real financial exposure that thousands of Georgia drivers carry unknowingly every time they get behind the wheel — simply because no one took the time to explain what their coverage actually does.

Our team explains this clearly, every time. We do not just sell you a policy with minimum limits and move on. We make sure you understand what those limits mean in practice so you can make an informed decision about the coverage that is right for your situation.

What your policy covers

Understanding Your Georgia Auto Insurance Coverage

Auto insurance is made up of several separate coverage types. Each one protects against different scenarios. Understanding what each does — and what it does not do — is the only way to make an informed decision about your policy.

Most misunderstood

Bodily Injury Liability

Pays for the other person's medical costs, lost wages, and pain and suffering when you cause an accident. Does not cover you or your passengers. Your limit is the maximum your insurance will pay — anything beyond that is your personal liability.

Property Damage Liability

Pays to repair or replace another person's vehicle or property when you are at fault. Georgia's state minimum is $25,000 — which may not cover a newer vehicle. If the damage exceeds your limit, you pay the remainder personally.

Commonly skipped

Uninsured & Underinsured Motorist

Protects you when the at-fault driver has no insurance or not enough. Covers your medical bills, lost wages, and damages. One of the most important coverages on the road — and one of the most commonly skipped. More on this below.

Collision Coverage

Pays to repair or replace your vehicle when it is damaged in a collision — with another vehicle, a guardrail, a pole, or any other object. Applies regardless of who is at fault. Required by most lenders if you have a car loan or lease.

Often misunderstood

Comprehensive Coverage

Covers damage from events other than collisions — theft, vandalism, weather, falling objects, hitting a deer, and most commonly, a cracked or shattered windshield. Because these claims tend to be smaller and more frequent, a lower deductible often makes financial sense here.

Medical Payments

Covers medical expenses for you and your passengers after an accident — regardless of fault. Works alongside health insurance and can help cover deductibles, copays, and expenses your health plan does not. A relatively affordable add-on that provides meaningful protection.

A coverage most people skip

Uninsured and Underinsured Motorist Coverage — Why It Matters More Than You Think

Georgia has a significant number of uninsured drivers on its roads. If one of them hits you and causes serious injuries, their lack of insurance becomes your problem — unless you have uninsured motorist coverage.

Even drivers who are insured often carry only the state minimum. If they cause an accident that results in $80,000 in medical bills and lost wages for you, their $25,000 policy covers less than a third of it. Underinsured motorist coverage pays the gap between what their policy covers and what your actual losses are.

This is coverage that protects you from other people's decisions — decisions you have no control over. It is one of the most important protections on the road and one of the most commonly declined because people do not fully understand what it does.

Our team explains both coverages clearly and helps you decide on limits that make sense for your household.

Ask Us About UM/UIM Coverage
Uninsured Motorist (UM)

Applies when the driver who hits you carries no insurance at all. Covers your medical bills, lost wages, and related damages — the costs you would otherwise have to pursue through a lawsuit against someone who may have nothing to collect from.

Underinsured Motorist (UIM)

Applies when the at-fault driver has insurance but not enough to cover your actual losses. Your UIM coverage fills the gap — up to your own policy limits. Without it, you absorb whatever their policy does not cover.

What Limits Should You Carry?

Your UM/UIM limits should generally match your liability limits — meaning if you are covered for serious injuries you cause to others, you should be equally covered for serious injuries others cause to you. Our team helps you think through this specifically for your household.

Two coverages — very different situations

Comprehensive vs. Collision — What Each One Actually Covers

Most drivers understand collision coverage. Comprehensive is where the confusion usually lives — and where the deductible strategy often gets it wrong.

Collision Coverage

When Your Car Hits Something

Collision coverage pays to repair or replace your vehicle when it is damaged by contact with another vehicle or object — regardless of who caused the accident.

Rear-ended by another driver
Sideswiped in traffic
Hitting a guardrail or pole
Single-vehicle accident
Comprehensive Coverage

When the World Happens to Your Car

Comprehensive covers damage from events outside a collision — things that happen to your vehicle, not things your vehicle runs into. Claims tend to be smaller and more frequent.

Cracked or shattered windshield — most common claim
Theft or vandalism
Hail, flooding, or storm damage
Hitting an animal — deer, etc.
Falling tree branches or objects
Deductible strategy: Because comprehensive claims tend to be small — windshields being the most frequent — carrying a lower deductible on comprehensive coverage often makes more financial sense than many drivers assume. A $100 or $250 comprehensive deductible vs. a $1,000 collision deductible is a strategy worth discussing with your agent.
What to watch for

Common Auto Coverage Gaps Georgia Drivers Miss

These are the gaps our team most commonly finds when reviewing existing auto policies — situations where drivers thought they were covered and were not.

State Minimum Liability Limits

Georgia's $25,000/$50,000/$25,000 minimums sound adequate until you understand what serious accidents actually cost. Carrying only minimum limits leaves you personally exposed to lawsuits, wage garnishment, and asset liens when a claim exceeds your coverage.

No Uninsured Motorist Coverage

Many drivers decline UM/UIM coverage to save money on their premium — not realizing it is one of the most important protections they can carry. Georgia roads have a significant number of uninsured drivers. If one hits you, UM coverage is the only thing that protects your financial recovery.

Mismatched Deductibles

Many drivers carry the same deductible on both collision and comprehensive without realizing the two cover very different types of claims. A high comprehensive deductible rarely makes sense given that most comprehensive claims — particularly windshields — fall well below a $500 or $1,000 threshold.

New Vehicle — Old Coverage Limits

Drivers who buy or lease a new vehicle sometimes forget to update their coverage limits accordingly. A policy written for a five-year-old car may not adequately cover the value of a new one — especially if gap insurance is not included and the vehicle is financed.

Teenage Drivers Not Listed

Adding a teenage driver to your household without updating your policy can create serious coverage gaps. Many parents assume their teen is automatically covered — but unlisted regular drivers can complicate or void claims. This conversation should happen before your teenager starts driving, not after.

Rental and Rideshare Gaps

Standard auto policies typically exclude vehicles used for rideshare services like Uber or Lyft, and rental coverage is often limited or not included by default. If you drive for a rideshare platform or rent vehicles regularly, these gaps are worth a direct conversation with your agent.

Common questions

Auto Insurance Questions — Answered Plainly

These are the questions Georgia drivers most commonly ask our team. Answered without jargon.

What is the minimum auto insurance required in Georgia?
Georgia requires $25,000 per person / $50,000 per accident for bodily injury, and $25,000 for property damage. These minimums are widely considered insufficient for serious accidents. If your coverage limits are exhausted by a claim, you are personally responsible for the remainder — which means lawsuits, wage garnishment, and potential asset seizure are real possibilities.
What does liability insurance actually cover?
Liability pays for the other party's costs when you cause an accident — their medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, and property damage, up to your policy limits. It does not cover your own vehicle or your own injuries. Your limit is the ceiling. Everything beyond it is your personal financial responsibility.
Can someone sue me personally after a car accident in Georgia?
Yes. If you cause an accident and the other party's losses exceed your liability limits, they can pursue you personally for the balance. Georgia law allows judgments that can result in wage garnishment and liens against your property. This is one of the most important reasons to carry liability limits well above the state minimum.
What is uninsured motorist coverage and do I need it?
Uninsured motorist (UM) coverage pays your medical bills and losses if you are hit by a driver with no insurance. Underinsured motorist (UIM) applies when the at-fault driver has insurance but not enough. Georgia has a significant uninsured driver population, making UM/UIM one of the most valuable coverages you can carry — and one of the most commonly skipped.
What is the difference between comprehensive and collision?
Collision covers damage from accidents — hitting another vehicle or object. Comprehensive covers damage from everything else — theft, weather, vandalism, animals, and cracked windshields (the most common comprehensive claim). Because comprehensive claims tend to be smaller and more frequent, a lower deductible on comprehensive coverage often makes more financial sense than drivers realize.
How can I lower my auto insurance premium in Georgia?
Bundling home and auto insurance, maintaining a clean driving record, completing a defensive driving course, and adjusting your deductibles strategically can all reduce your premium. Our team reviews your specific situation to find the discounts that actually apply to you — not a generic checklist applied to everyone.
