What the Data Shows

Georgia Is Not a Mild-Weather State

Most people associate catastrophic storm damage with Florida or the Gulf Coast. The data tells a different story for Georgia. The state averages 25 to 40 tornadoes per year according to NOAA severe weather climatology records, with peak activity running from March through May — and severe weather season that is essentially year-round. In 2024 alone, Georgia experienced an outbreak of more than 79 tornadoes causing approximately $2.4 billion in property damage. Hurricane Helene added an estimated $10 to $20 billion more in associated costs to the state that same year.

Wind and hail are the single largest driver of homeowners insurance claims nationwide — accounting for 42.5% of all claims filed between 2019 and 2023, with an average payout of $14,747 per claim according to Insurance Information Institute data. Georgia's position in the Southeast storm corridor means that figure is not an abstraction. It is the most likely reason a Georgia homeowner will ever file a claim.

What many homeowners do not know is that their policy's response to storm damage may look different than they expect. Georgia insurers are increasingly applying separate wind and hail deductibles that can reach 5% of a home's insured value — meaning a homeowner with a $400,000 dwelling could owe $20,000 out of pocket before coverage applies. Carriers are also restricting or declining coverage on roofs older than 15 to 20 years, regardless of condition. These are not hypotheticals. They are the specifics that determine whether a claim results in full repair or a significant personal financial loss.

What a Coverage Review Actually Checks

We review your dwelling limits against current replacement costs — not the purchase price. We identify separate wind, hail, and hurricane deductibles. We flag roof age restrictions that could affect your claim outcome. And we discuss flood coverage, which standard homeowners policies do not include.

A review takes 20 to 30 minutes and costs nothing. The gaps it finds can be significant.

Georgia Auto Insurance Facts

Nearly 1 in 5 Georgia Drivers Has No Insurance

Georgia's uninsured driver rate sits at approximately 19 percent — well above the national average of 15 percent, according to Insurance Information Institute data. That means on any given road in Georgia, roughly 1 in 5 vehicles carries no liability coverage. If an uninsured driver causes an accident that injures you or damages your vehicle, their policy will not pay — because they do not have one.

The Georgia Department of Transportation recorded 367,523 crashes in 2024, resulting in 1,494 fatal crashes and 1,596 fatalities. Over the five-year period from 2020 through 2024, the state saw more than 1.8 million total crashes and 8,451 fatal crashes. These are not outliers. They are what driving in Georgia looks like at scale.

Uninsured motorist coverage closes the gap when an at-fault driver has no policy to pay for your damages. It is one of the most important — and most underutilized — coverages available to Georgia drivers. We discuss it with every auto insurance client, not as an upsell, but as a straightforward response to a real and documented risk on Georgia roads.

Common Questions

Georgia Insurance Questions — Answered Directly

How many tornadoes does Georgia get per year?
Georgia averages 25 to 40 tornadoes per year according to NOAA and Georgia severe weather climatology records, with peak activity in spring — March through May. In 2024, Georgia experienced an outbreak of more than 79 tornadoes that caused approximately $2.4 billion in property damage.
Does my homeowners insurance cover wind and hail damage in Georgia?
Standard policies typically cover wind and hail, but Georgia insurers are increasingly adding separate wind and hail deductibles as high as 5% of your home's insured value. For a $400,000 home, that is $20,000 out of pocket before coverage applies. We review deductible structures during every home insurance consultation.
What percentage of Georgia drivers are uninsured?
Approximately 19% of Georgia drivers are uninsured — above the 15% national average. That means nearly 1 in 5 vehicles on Georgia roads carries no insurance. Uninsured motorist coverage protects you when an at-fault driver has no policy to pay your damages.
Does homeowners insurance cover flooding in Georgia?
No. Standard homeowners insurance does not cover flood damage. Flood coverage requires a separate flood insurance policy. More than 20% of flood claims are filed by homeowners outside FEMA-designated high-risk flood zones — making flood risk relevant for far more Georgia homeowners than those in obvious flood plains.
Why did my Georgia home insurance rate go up?
Georgia homeowners insurance rates rose nearly 12% in the past year, driven primarily by increased storm severity, rising construction costs, and inflation affecting repair and replacement expenses. Rates have climbed approximately 40% nationally since 2019. We can review your current policy to help you understand what is driving your rate and whether your coverage still makes sense.

Yes. Our office is in Downtown Buford, Georgia, and we serve homeowners, families, drivers, and business owners throughout the state. We handle quotes and coverage reviews by phone and email — no office visit required.
