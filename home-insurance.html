What is covered

What a Georgia Homeowners Policy Typically Covers

Standard homeowners insurance is made up of several coverage types that work together. Understanding each one helps you make informed decisions about your limits and deductibles.

Dwelling Coverage

Covers the cost to repair or rebuild your home's physical structure if it is damaged by a covered event such as fire, wind, hail, or lightning. Your dwelling limit should reflect the full cost to rebuild your home at today's construction prices — not what you paid for it.

Personal Property

Covers your furniture, clothing, electronics, appliances, and other belongings inside your home. Most policies offer actual cash value by default — we recommend reviewing whether replacement cost coverage makes sense for your household.

Liability Coverage

Protects you if someone is injured on your property or if you accidentally damage someone else's property. This coverage also helps pay for legal defense if you are sued. Standard limits may not be enough — especially for homeowners with pools, trampolines, or dogs.

Other Structures

Covers detached structures on your property such as a separate garage, fence, storage shed, or gazebo. This coverage is typically calculated as a percentage of your dwelling limit — worth reviewing if you have significant outbuildings.

Loss of Use

Also called Additional Living Expenses (ALE), this coverage pays for temporary housing, meals, and other costs if your home becomes uninhabitable due to a covered loss. Many homeowners underestimate how quickly these costs can add up after a major event.

What Is Not Covered

Standard policies typically exclude flood damage, earthquake damage, sewer backups, and normal wear and tear. In Georgia, flood coverage requires a separate policy through the NFIP or a private carrier. Our team will help you identify any coverage gaps that apply to your property.

What to watch for

Common Coverage Gaps Georgia Homeowners Miss

A policy that looks complete on paper can leave significant exposure in practice. These are the gaps our team most commonly finds when reviewing existing policies.

Underinsured Dwelling Limits

Construction costs have risen sharply in recent years. Many homeowners are carrying dwelling limits that were set years ago and no longer reflect the actual cost to rebuild. If your home is destroyed, an outdated limit could leave you significantly short of what you need to rebuild to the same standard.

Actual Cash Value vs. Replacement Cost

Policies that pay actual cash value deduct for depreciation — meaning a 10-year-old roof might pay out far less than what a new roof actually costs. Replacement cost coverage costs more upfront but protects you far better when you actually need to make a claim.

No Flood Coverage

Flood damage is not covered by standard homeowners insurance in Georgia. Many homeowners outside designated flood zones assume they do not need it — but heavy rain events regularly cause flood damage in areas with no official flood risk designation. This gap can be catastrophic and is worth a direct conversation.

Insufficient Liability Limits

The standard $100,000 liability limit in many policies may not be enough if a serious injury occurs on your property. Homeowners with pools, trampolines, or dogs face elevated liability exposure and should consider whether higher limits or an umbrella policy makes sense.

Home Business Exposure

If you run any kind of business from your home — including remote work, client meetings, or inventory storage — your standard homeowners policy may not cover business-related losses or liability. This is an increasingly common gap as more Georgians work from home.

Jewelry, Art, and High-Value Items

Most standard policies cap coverage on specific categories of valuables such as jewelry, fine art, collectibles, and firearms. If you own items in these categories, a scheduled personal property endorsement may be needed to adequately protect them.

Common questions

Home Insurance Questions — Answered Honestly

These are the questions Georgia homeowners most commonly ask our team. We have answered them plainly, without insurance jargon.

How much does home insurance cost in Georgia?
Georgia home insurance rates vary based on your home's age, location, construction type, claims history, and the coverage limits you choose. Rates have increased in recent years due to inflation and weather-related claims. The best way to understand what you will pay is to have a local agent review your specific property — not just run an online quote that doesn't account for your home's actual characteristics.
What does homeowners insurance cover in Georgia?
A standard Georgia homeowners policy covers your home's structure (dwelling), attached structures, personal belongings, liability if someone is injured on your property, and additional living expenses if your home becomes uninhabitable due to a covered loss. Flood, earthquake, and sewer backup are typically excluded and require separate coverage.
Is flood insurance required in Georgia?
Flood insurance is not part of a standard homeowners policy. If your home is in a FEMA-designated flood zone, your mortgage lender will likely require it. Even outside flood zones, Georgia homeowners should seriously consider flood coverage — heavy rain events cause flooding far beyond officially designated risk areas, and a single flood event without coverage can be financially devastating.
What is the difference between actual cash value and replacement cost coverage?
Actual cash value (ACV) pays what your property is worth at the time of the loss, after depreciation. If your 12-year-old roof is destroyed, ACV might pay a fraction of what a new roof costs. Replacement cost coverage pays what it actually costs to replace or rebuild at current prices. The premium difference is usually modest — but the difference in what you receive after a major claim can be substantial.
Can I lower my home insurance premium in Georgia?
Yes. Bundling home and auto insurance, updating your roof or systems, installing security equipment, and reviewing your deductible structure can all reduce your premium. Our team reviews each client's situation individually to identify the discounts that apply to their specific home — not a generic checklist.
Do I need home insurance if I own my home outright?
Georgia law does not require home insurance if you have no mortgage. But going without it means you are personally responsible for the full cost to rebuild after a fire, replace all your belongings after a theft, or pay a liability judgment if someone is injured on your property. For most homeowners, those risks far exceed the cost of coverage.
